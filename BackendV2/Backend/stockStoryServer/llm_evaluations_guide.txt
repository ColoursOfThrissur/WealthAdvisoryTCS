Stock Story Writer – LLM Evaluation Guide (Non-Technical)

This document explains, in simple terms, where and how we evaluate the AI (LLM) inside the Stock Story Writer. It focuses only on the AI’s behavior, not low-level technical tests.


1. Overview of the user flow

In a typical session, a user:
1) Tells the chatbot which company and date range they care about.
2) The AI gathers price data and news in the background.
3) The AI shows weekly summaries of what happened.
4) The AI generates a final stock story.
5) The user may ask follow-up questions or request more stories.

We place LLM evaluations at key points in this journey.


1a. Key evaluation names (at a glance)

For quick reference, here are the main LLM evaluations we run and where they appear:
- Evaluation A – Request Understanding:
  - Checks whether the AI correctly understands the user’s company, date range, and intent, and asks for clarification when needed.
- Evaluation B – Step Explanation:
  - Checks whether the AI clearly explains the next step before it does anything important behind the scenes.
- Evaluation C – Weekly Summary Quality:
  - Checks whether each weekly summary is concise, accurate, and focused on what matters for the stock.
- Evaluation D – Final Stock Story Quality:
  - Checks whether the final story is coherent, insightful, and written in the right professional tone.
- Evaluation E – Follow-up Answer Quality:
  - Checks whether answers to extra questions are consistent with the story and genuinely helpful.
- Evaluation F – Guardrails & Off-Scope Handling:
  - Checks whether the AI politely refuses or redirects when a request falls outside the intended use of the system.


2. Evaluation Point A – Understanding the user’s request

Where in the flow:
- Right after the user types their first message (for example: “Create a stock story for Infosys from Sept to Dec 2025”) and every time they change or clarify the request.

What we check:
- Clarity of interpretation:
  - Does the AI correctly understand which company and which date range the user wants?
  - If information is missing, does it ask for the right clarification instead of guessing?
- Staying on-purpose:
  - If the user asks for something unrelated to stock stories, does the AI gently steer them back to “company + date range” instead of wandering off-topic?

How we evaluate:
- We keep a small set of example user messages and look at:
  - Whether the AI’s follow-up questions are clear and minimal.
  - How often humans say: “Yes, it understood what I meant” vs. “No, it misunderstood.”


3. Evaluation Point B – Explaining the next step before doing it

Where in the flow:
- Each time the AI is about to move into an important new phase:
  - After finding significant weeks in the stock data.
  - Before starting the news analysis for those weeks.
  - Before generating the final story.

What we check:
- Transparency:
  - Does the AI clearly tell the user what it is about to do next in plain language?
  - Example: “Now I’m going to look at news articles for the weeks where the stock moved the most, and I’ll show you short summaries.”
- User confidence:
  - Do users feel they understand what is happening “behind the scenes” enough to trust the output?

How we evaluate:
- Review short transcripts and rate:
  - How understandable the “next step” explanations are (e.g., 1–5 scale).
  - Whether users say they feel in control and informed.


4. Evaluation Point C – Quality of weekly summaries

Where in the flow:
- After the AI has gathered news for each important week and produces the short weekly summaries that show up in the carousel in the UI.

What we check:
- Usefulness:
  - Does each weekly summary capture the core news that matters for the stock, rather than minor or cosmetic events?
  - Can a human quickly understand “what actually happened that week” from the summary?
- Brevity and focus:
  - Is the summary short enough to scan quickly, without unnecessary fluff?
  - Does it stick to business and financial impact, not general marketing language?
- Groundedness:
  - Is the summary clearly based on the articles it cites (no obvious made‑up facts)?

How we evaluate:
- For a sample of weeks:
  - Humans compare the summary against the original articles and score:
    - “Does this feel accurate?” (Yes/No or 1–5).
    - “Does this focus on the right things for investors?” (1–5).
  - Optionally, a second AI model can act as a reviewer, checking whether each claim appears in the provided article text.


5. Evaluation Point D – Quality of the final stock story

Where in the flow:
- After the user has accepted the weekly summaries and the AI produces the single narrative stock story for the full period.

What we check:
- Narrative and structure:
  - Does the story read like a coherent, chronological explanation of the quarter, not a random list of facts?
  - Does it connect the events across weeks into a clear “story arc”?
- Style and tone:
  - Does it sound like a professional, neutral financial summary (Reuters/WSJ-like), not like marketing copy or casual chat?
  - Does it avoid forbidden patterns (for example, explicit price percentages if that’s a rule)?
- Decision support:
  - After reading the story, could an investor or analyst say, “I have a decent high-level understanding of how this stock performed and why”?

How we evaluate:
- Keep a small benchmark set of final stories for known periods:
  - Human evaluators (PMs, analysts, or power users) rate each story on:
    - Clarity and coherence.
    - Insightfulness: does it highlight the truly important drivers?
    - Trustworthiness: does it feel grounded and unbiased?
  - Optionally, a reviewer AI model can check if the story:
    - Follows the requested tone.
    - Avoids clear contradictions with the weekly summaries it is based on.


6. Evaluation Point E – Answering follow-up questions

Where in the flow:
- After the main story is generated, when a user asks extra questions like:
  - “Why did the stock move so sharply in November?”
  - “How did management guidance change over this period?”

What we check:
- Consistency with the story:
  - Do follow-up answers stay consistent with the original story and weekly summaries, instead of contradicting them?
- Depth without overreach:
  - Does the AI answer within what it reasonably knows from the data and summaries, instead of speculating wildly?
- Helpfulness:
  - Do users feel that follow-up answers actually clarify their questions, rather than restating the story in a vague way?

How we evaluate:
- Take a sample of “story + follow-up questions + answers”:
  - Ask humans to rate whether the answers are:
    - Consistent with the story.
    - Specific enough to be useful.
  - Optionally, an AI reviewer checks for contradictions between answers and the original story.


7. Evaluation Point F – Handling off-scope or unsafe requests

Where in the flow:
- Whenever the user asks for something outside the intended use:
  - Non-stock questions (“help me write my CV”).
  - Requests that look like they want personal financial advice rather than descriptive stories.

What we check:
- Guardrails:
  - Does the AI decline or redirect gracefully, using the agreed-upon wording?
  - Does it remind the user what it can and cannot do?
- User trust:
  - Does this behavior increase user trust (the system knows its limits) rather than frustrate them?

How we evaluate:
- Create a set of “red flag” prompts and see:
  - How often the AI responds with the correct refusal / redirection message.
  - Whether the language of those refusals feels polite and clear to non-technical users.


8. Putting it all together

In practice, we do not evaluate every single interaction. Instead, we:
- Maintain a small library of example conversations that cover key patterns (normal requests, edge cases, off-scope requests).
- Regularly sample real conversations from production and review them with the lenses above.
- Use a mix of:
  - Human reviewers (product, domain experts, or pilot users).
  - Secondary AI models acting as “judges” to quickly flag potential issues.
